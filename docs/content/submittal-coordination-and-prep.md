# Submittal Coordination & Submittal Prep — The Hidden Gap in Construction Procurement

## The Industry Problem

When a contractor awards a subcontract or purchase order, one of the first questions asked is: *"How long until we get submittals?"*

The vendor answers something like: *"Six weeks."*

The contractor writes "6 weeks" on their schedule and moves on. But the contractor and the vendor are talking about two different things.

**What the vendor means:** Six weeks from the moment they have every piece of information they need — design details confirmed, RFIs answered, interface dimensions from other trades resolved, product selections finalized — to produce a complete submittal package.

**What the contractor assumes:** Six weeks from contract execution.

The time between contract execution and the vendor having all the information they need is **Submittal Coordination**. It can take days on simple items and weeks or months on complex ones. Most contractors never explicitly plan for it. That unplanned gap is one of the primary drivers of procurement-driven schedule drift in construction.

---

## Submittal Coordination

Submittal Coordination is the collaborative effort required to get the vendor everything they need before they can begin producing submittals. It is not administrative overhead — it is real, active work involving multiple parties.

### What happens during this phase

- **Vendor RFIs.** The awarded vendor reviews the contract documents and identifies gaps, conflicts, or ambiguities. They submit Requests for Information to the contractor, who routes them to the architect, engineer, or owner as needed. Each RFI may require coordination across disciplines before it can be answered.

- **Multi-disciplinary review.** Complex procurement items don't exist in isolation. Structural steel connections affect curtain wall attachment details. Mechanical equipment dimensions affect the structural framing that supports them. Electrical rough-in depends on equipment cut sheets that don't exist yet. During coordination, these interdependencies are identified and resolved — or at least acknowledged — before the vendor commits to producing drawings.

- **Input from other vendors.** The awarded vendor often cannot finalize their submittals without information from other trades. The steel fabricator needs the curtain wall vendor's embed locations. The millwork vendor needs the stone vendor's countertop dimensions. The mechanical sub needs the controls vendor's sequence of operations. The contractor is responsible for orchestrating this cross-vendor coordination.

- **Design clarifications and revisions.** Coordination often reveals that the design documents are incomplete, inconsistent, or unbuildable as drawn. The architect or engineer may need to issue supplemental sketches, revised details, or formal addenda before the vendor can proceed. This takes time, and the contractor has no control over it.

- **Owner decisions.** In some cases, coordination stalls because the owner has not made a required selection or approved a design direction. A vendor cannot begin producing submittals for custom cabinetry if the owner hasn't chosen the wood species, finish, and hardware. This is where Final Design and Final Selection constraints come into play — they make visible the fact that the timeline is waiting on someone outside the contractor's control.

### Why contractors underestimate this phase

1. **It's invisible in traditional scheduling.** Most procurement schedules have one line item: "Submittals." There is no separate duration for the work that must happen before the vendor can start.

2. **Vendors don't quote it.** When a vendor says "6 weeks for submittals," they are quoting their production time. They are not quoting the time they'll spend waiting for answers, chasing information from other trades, or sitting idle while the architect resolves a design conflict.

3. **It varies dramatically by item.** A standard hollow metal door frame might need zero coordination — the vendor can produce submittals straight from the contract documents. A custom unitized curtain wall system might require months of coordination involving the structural engineer, the architect, the waterproofing consultant, and three other trades before a single shop drawing can be started.

4. **Nobody owns it explicitly.** Coordination falls into a gray area between the contractor's project management team, the vendor, the design team, and sometimes the owner. Without explicit planning, it happens reactively — and reactive coordination is slow coordination.

## Submittal Prep

Submittal Prep is the vendor's work to produce the actual submittal package. This phase begins only after Submittal Coordination is complete — meaning the vendor has all information, answers, and inputs required.

### What happens during this phase

- **Shop drawings.** The vendor produces detailed fabrication-level drawings showing exactly what they intend to build, including dimensions, materials, connections, finishes, and installation details. These translate the architect's design intent into buildable reality.

- **Product data.** Manufacturer cut sheets, specification sheets, performance data, test reports, and compliance documentation for every product or material included in the scope.

- **Samples.** Physical samples of materials, finishes, colors, or assemblies as required by the specifications. These are prepared and shipped to the contractor or architect for review.

- **Calculations.** Where required, the vendor provides engineering calculations — structural, thermal, acoustic, or other performance calculations that demonstrate compliance with the design requirements.

- **Internal QA.** Before submitting to the contractor, the vendor's internal team reviews the package for completeness, accuracy, and compliance. A good vendor catches their own errors before the architect has to.

### The contractor's role during this phase

The contractor is largely waiting, but not passive. The PM or PE should be:

- Tracking progress against the committed prep duration
- Remaining available to answer any last-minute questions that arise during production
- Confirming that the submittal package requirements (number of copies, format, required contents) are clear
- Preparing for the review phase that follows

## Why JiTpro Separates These Two Phases

By requiring the user to enter separate durations for Submittal Coordination and Submittal Prep, JiTpro forces a behavioral change:

1. **It makes the hidden work visible.** A contractor who has to enter a coordination duration must actually think about how long that effort will take. They can no longer hide it inside the vendor's quoted lead time.

2. **It creates accountability.** When coordination runs long, the baseline comparison shows exactly where the delay occurred — in coordination, not in the vendor's production time. This matters when explaining schedule impacts to owners and architects, especially when the delay was caused by slow RFI responses or missing design information.

3. **It improves forecasting.** A PM who has tracked coordination durations across multiple projects starts to build intuition: *"Last time we did a custom curtain wall, coordination took 8 weeks before the vendor could start. We should plan for that again."*

4. **It protects the contractor.** When an owner or architect asks why a procurement item is behind schedule, the contractor can point to the data: *"Coordination took 6 weeks longer than planned because we were waiting on design clarifications. The vendor's actual production time was on schedule."* The timeline tells the story with dates, not opinions.

## Real-World Example

**Item: Custom Structural Steel**

| Phase | Planned Duration | What Actually Happened |
|-------|-----------------|------------------------|
| Buyout | 2 weeks | Contract executed on time |
| Submittal Coordination | 4 weeks | Took 9 weeks — architect revised connection details twice, MEP coordination required 3 rounds of input from mechanical sub |
| Submittal Prep | 6 weeks | Vendor produced submittals in 5 weeks (ahead of schedule once they had everything) |
| 1st Review | 2 weeks | Architect took 3 weeks |

Without separating coordination from prep, this project would show "submittals took 14 weeks instead of 6." The contractor looks slow. With JiTpro's separation, the data shows coordination took 9 weeks instead of 4 — the design team caused the delay — and the vendor actually beat their quoted prep time by a week. The accountability lands where it belongs.
